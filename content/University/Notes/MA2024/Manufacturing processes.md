# Polymer Engineering
### Fundamentals of polymers
- A polymer is a **large** molecule (high molecular weight) constructed from many smaller structural **repetitive units** (monomers), **covalently bonded** together (crosslinks along the single polymer chain)
    
    ![[References/Images/image 10 3.png|image 10 3.png]]
    
Polymerization: Monomers are activated and react with each other creating chains
- The reaction is hard to control
- Molecules bind to closest neighbours
- Molecules formed are massive and can be poly-dispersed
Plastics = Polymers with **Additives**
- Two big families of polymers → Thermoplastics & Thermosets
    
    ![[References/Images/image 11 3.png|image 11 3.png]]
    
      
    
- **Thermosets** have **crosslinks** that do not break with heating
    
    Two key types are listed below
    
    ![[References/Images/image 12 3.png|image 12 3.png]]
    
- **Thermoplastics** molecules slide when heated above glass transition temperature (**reptation**)
    
    ![[References/Images/image 13 3.png|image 13 3.png]]
    
### Properties of polymer melt
- **Viscosity** is the resistance to flow
    - The viscosity of a polymer melt decreases with shear rate, and therefore becomes more fluid at higher shear rate. This is called **shear-thinning** or **pseudoplastic fluid** behavior
    - Viscosity also decreases with temperature
    - Shear history also decreases viscosity
- **Viscoelasticity**: Delayed reaction to stress
### Polymer forming process
Processes are applied during soft, liquid form
Casting - Extrusion - Injection Molding - Compression Molding - Transfer Molding - Blow Molding - Thermoforming

### Extrusion

> [!important] **Extrusion** is a continuous process for making profile shapes (rods, fibres, tubes, etc.), films and sheets or parison (the preform for blow molding)
- Single-Screw extruder
    
    ![[References/Images/image 14 3.png|image 14 3.png]]
    
- Shear Heating is the main mechanism for melting the polymer
Process flow:
Polymer pellet feeding → Screw convey pellet into heating zone → Friction + Heaters melt pellet → Liquid polymer pass through die → Desired shape extruded
Process Zones:
- Feed zone: Constant screw depth - to preheat and convey to next zone
- Compression zone: Decreasing screw depth - To squeeze trapped air back to feed zone - Improving heat transfer
- Metering zone: Constant screw depth - Homogenized melting here to provide constant melted polymer
- Filter & Breaker plates: Straighten the spiralling flow - Create back pressure, better mixing
Extrusion defects:
- Die swell
    
    ![[References/Images/image 15 3.png|image 15 3.png]]
    
    - At a fixed shear rate, increasing die length decreases die swell.
    - Die swell from a slit is generally larger than from a capillary.
    - It is important to design the correct exit shape to obtain the desired product.
- Shark Skin & Melt Fracture
    
    ![[References/Images/image 16 3.png|image 16 3.png]]
    
    - The distortion of the extrudate is induced by high shear stresses at the walls

# Metal forming
## Bulk Deformation Process
Rolling - Forging - Extrusion - Wire and Bar drawing

> [!important] To induce shape change on workpiece using plastic deformation via force application
Merits:
- Produces common shapes inexpensively
- Can achieve significant shape changes in the workpiece while maintaining good mechanical properties
- Produces little or no waste byproduct
- Achieves final product geometry with minimal or no post-machining required
### Hot/Cold Rolling

> [!important]
> 
> - Thickness of workpiece is reduced by compressive force exerted by two opposing rolls
>     
>     ![[References/Images/image 36 2.png|image 36 2.png]]
>     
Hot rolling:
- 850–1200°C for steel
- When massive deformation required
- End product = No residual stress + isotropic properties
- Limitations:
    - difficult to achieve close tolerances
    - surface exhibit oxide scale
Cold rolling:
- 60 - 180°C
- Less severe deformation required
- Mechanical properties improved
    - Strength increased through strain hardening
- Good finishing

- High strength
- Good dimensional accuracy
- Good surface finish

$$Q=A\times V=constant$$
Q = volumetric flow rate in $cm^3/s$
A = Cross sectional area in $cm^2$
v = velocity of flow in cm/s
![[image 59.png]]

$$v_2=\sqrt{2gh}$$

> [!important] Significance: To tune mold design to allow laminar flow, ensuring proper filling of mold cavity
Reynolds number — Laminar < 2000 — Turbulent > 20000
$$Re=\frac{\rho vd}{\eta}$$
$\rho = density$
v = velocity of fluid flow
d = diameter of channel
$\eta =viscosity$

### Fluidity - capability to fill mold casting

$$T_{TS}=C_m(\frac{V}{A})^n$$
$T_{TS}$= total solidification time
A= surface area of casting
V= volume of casting
$C_m$= mold constant
n = exponent with typical value = 2

> [!important] It tells us: 1) How much metal is needed to fill the mold — 2) time taken to solidify

## Metal casting process
### Expendable mold
Sand casting
- Poor dimensional accuracy and tolerances
- Simple and widely used
Investment casting (lost wax process)
Wax pattern is coated with refractory material then melted away for a precise mold
- Mold is heated to a high temperature allowing contaminant are eliminated and liquid metal flows easily
- Mold is broken away after
- Advantages: Complex parts — Accurate & good surface finish — reuse wax — no additional machining
- Disadvantages: Many processing steps — expensive
### Permanent mold
- Mold is openable — preheat and coat — cores inserted before closing and pouring
- Advantages: Accurate & good surface finish — stronger castings due to finer grains
- Disadvantages: Low melting point metals only — Simple geometry — Expensive mold

## Chip types and surface finish
### Chip types
Continuous chips
- Ductile material @ high speed
- good surface finish
- tangle around workstation

> Change machining parameters or chip breakers to remedy
Discontinuous chips
- Brittle material / hard inclusions or impurities present
- Cutting force varies during cutting
Built-up edge (BUE)
1. Layer of material get deposited on the tool until it breaks up
2. Part of it leaves with the chip and the rest is deposited on the w/p, creating a rough finish
3. The depositing on the tool changing the cutting edge and ruins the surface finish
Serrated chips
- Saw tooth like appearance due to strength that decrease with temperature, e.g. titanium
### Surface finish
Roughness parameter:
$R_a = \text{average deviation from center}$
$R_t = \text{distance between highest peak and lowest valley}$
Factors affecting
1. Feed marks left by cutting operations
    
    $$R_a = \frac{f^2}{32R} \ \ \ \ \ \ \  
    R_t = \frac{f^2}{8R}$$
    
    f = feed (mm/rev) R = tool nose radium (mm)
    
2. Vibration
    1. Force vibration — periodic vibration due to tool usage
    2. Self-excited vibration — due to disturbance of cutting zone - could leading to chipping / premature failure of cutting tool

# Metal Joining
## Fundamental of Welding

> [!important] Joining parts with heat or pressure
### Type of welding
Fusion welding — Join by melting base material
- Filler material can be added to facilitate process and increase bulk / strength
- **Oxyfuel gas welding (OFW)**
    - Melt using oxyfuel gas — e.g. acetylene
- **Arc welding (AW)**
    - Melt using electric arc
- **Laser Welding**
Solid state welding (SSW) — Join using pressure mainly or alone
- No melting, no filler metal
- **Forge welding**
- **Ultrasonic wire bonding**
### Physics of welding

> [!important] Aim: To melt metal with minimum energy but high power density
Power density (PD — W/mm^2)
$$PD = \frac{P}{A}$$
P = Power entering surface (W) A = Surface area energy entering (mm^2)
![[image 81.png]]
Unit energy for melting $U_m$ — To heat up + melt
$$U_m = kT_m^2$$
$T_m$ = Melting temp of material (K) $k=3.33*10^{-6}$
Heat transfer — Not all input energy used for welding
$$H_w = f_1f_2H$$
$H_w=\text{net heat available for welding}$ $H=\text{total heat generated by welding process}$
Heat transfer efficiency, $f_1$
- Heat received at work surface / heat generated at source
- Depends on source
Melting efficiency, $f_2$
- Heat used for melting / Heat received at work surface
- High thermal conductivity metals like Al and Cu have low efficiency here
Energy Balance Equation
$$H_w=U_mV$$
If time is considered:
$$HR_w=U_m(WVR)=f_1f_2HR=U_mA_wv$$
V = volume of metal melted (mm^3) WVR = Welding volume rate (mm^3/min)
$HR_w=\text{rate of energy delivered}$ $A_w = \text{weld area}$ $v=\text{welding speed}$
## Welding Process & Features
### Arc Welding — Fusion Welding

> [!important] Coalescence of metals by an electric arc between electrode and work
- ~5500 degree celcius — melt **any** metal
![[image 82.png]]
### Arc shielding - prevent metal from reacting with air in this process and degrade
- Shielding gases (Argon, helium, CO2 …)
- **Flux**
    - Prevent formation of oxides + dissolves oxide for removal
    - Provide protective atmosphere for welding
    - Stabalizes arc, reduces spattering
    - Application methods: Direct pouring, coated electrode, tubular electrode
- Submerged-Arc Welding

### Types of electrodes
Consumable electrodes
- Welding rods/sticks, must be changed often
- Weld wire, continuously fed from spools
- Both consumed and added as filler material
Non-Consumable electrodes
- **Tungsten** — resists melting — 3410 celcius
- gradually depleted by vaporization
- May not have filler
### Non-consumable electrode processes
Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW)
- Tungsten electrode + inert gas shielding
- AKA TIG welding (Tungsten Inert Gas Welding)
- Application: aluminum and stainless steel

## Brazing, Soldering and Adhesive Bonding
### Brazing
![[image 99.png]]
- Low melting pt, >450 celcius
- Only filler metal
Why brazing? — Poor weldability between metals (Dissimilar / metal + ceramics)
Brazed Joints design — **Maximise contact area**
### Soldering
- Same as brazing but lower than 450 celcius
- Filler metal = Solder
- Mainly for electronics assembly
### Adhesive Bonding AKA glue
- Can bond basically all materials
- Require maximised contact area for good joining
