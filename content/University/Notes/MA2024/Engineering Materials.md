### Density of Unit Cell (p)
$$p = \frac{n*A_m}{N_A*V_C}$$
n = atom per unit cell
$N_a$ = Avogadro’s number (6.023 x 10^23)
  
$A_m$ = Atomic mass
$V_C$ = Volume of unit cell
  
## Crystallographic Parameters
### Crystallographic Direction

> [!important] A vector connecting the coordinate origin and a point of a unit cell [u,v,w]
- Bar above number indicates negative value $[\bar{u},v,w]$
- Scale value to ≤ 1 for sketching
Families of directions : vectors with the same magnitude
- symbolised by <U V W>
- parallel directions are not unique
### Crystallographic Planes

> [!important] Set of parallel and equally spaced planes that pass through the centers of atoms in crystals where no centers are situated between planes

> **Miller indices:** Symbolic vector representation for the orientation of an atomic direction or plane in a crystal lattice
- Described as (h k l) where “h,k & l” are miller indices
- Metal deformation are dependent on sliding of these planes — transportation of heat and electrons are also affected by them
Miller indices for given plane
- Plane cannot touch origin / new origin — based on closest corner
- Find intercepts — reciprocal for plane miller indices
- E.g. intercept = -1 , -1/2, 1 → $(\bar{1}\space\bar{2}\space1)$
Draw plane given miller indices
- Reciprocal to find intercepts
Family of planes

> [!important] Crystallographically equivalent — Same packing density, atomic environment, physical & mechanical properties
- Indicated by {h k l}
- **For cubic systems only**, same indices = family
    - (100) (010) ($\bar1$,0,0) — same family {100}
### Linear Density
$$LD=\frac{No. \space of \space atoms \space centered \space on \space directional \space vector}{Length \space of \space directional \space vector}$$

> [!important] End point of vector does not cut the center
### Planar Density
$$PD=\frac{\text{No. of atoms centered on a plane}}{\text{Area of plane}}$$
- Only area of atom within the plane is considered
  
# Properties of Crystalline Solids
## Defects in Solids
### Point defects
Vacancies: site where atom is missing in lattice
Self-interstitial: ‘extra’ atom between lattice site

> [!important] The crystal structure aims to minimise strain, and thus the energy of the system (Gibbs free energy G), dependent on:
> 
> - Enthalpy (H): energy cost to break atomic bonds forming defect
> - Entropy (TS): energy associated with no. of possible states
$$G=H-TS$$
Equilibrium concentration of vacancies @ T = constant (≠0)
**ARRHENIUS LAW**
$$\frac{n}{N} = \huge e^{(-\frac{Q_v}{kT})}$$
n = number of defects — N = Number of atomic sites
$Q_v$ = activation energy of vacancy formation [J/mol.] or [eV/atom]
k = Boltzmann’s constant, 1.38 x 10^-23 [J/atom/K] or 8.62 x 10^-5 [eV/atom/K]

- Tetrahedral — 1/4 3/4 1/4
Interstitial site positions in BCC:
- Octahedral — 1/2 1 0

## Dislocations

> [!important] Plastic deformation is due to movement of dislocations — slipping in the direction of burgers vector
- Dislocations increase G proportionally to b^2
- Dislocations of the **same sign** repel while **opposite signs** attract and annihilate
![[References/Images/image 13 4.png|image 13 4.png]]
- Energy required to move dislocation is related to burgers vector b
    - Thus preferred direction is one with minimum b
### Slip systems
- **Slip planes** — easiest slippage due to high planar density
- **Slip directions —** along burgers vectors
![[References/Images/image 14 4.png|image 14 4.png]]
### Resolved shear stress $\tau_r$
![[References/Images/image 15 4.png|image 15 4.png]]
### Deformation of ceramics
- Most fracture before plastic deformation
- Brittle → ionic bonds → difficult to slip due to repulsive coulombic forces between + - ions
- ionic and covalent materials are brittle and display no plastic deformation

- Can increase with **Work Hardening / Strain Hardening**
    
    - Due to plastic deformation

Toughness: energy required to break a unit volume of material
- Brittle(low toughness) → high yield strength but %EL — ceramics
- Tough → good yield strength and %EL — metals
- Ductile(moderate toughness) → large %EL but very low yield strength — soft polymers
- **Brittle fracture — elastic energy dominant**
- **Ductile fracture — plastic energy dominant**
### True stress and strain

> [!important] In the engineering graph, it curves down as the cross-sectional area rapidly decreases within neck region at the strain limits, making the material appear to weaken when it is actually strengthening
True stress vs engineering stress: $\sigma_T = \sigma(1+\epsilon)$
True strain vs engineering strain: $\epsilon_T=ln(1+\epsilon)$

## Strengthening Mechanisms

> [!important] Design principle: Increase intrinsic resistance to dislocation motion to reduce plastic deformation
### Grain boundary strengthening

> [!important] At < 0.5 melting temperature, grain boundaries restrict dislocation motion as it requires more energy/stress to slip past them
Smaller grain size → more barriers → higher stress to move dislocations → higher strength
Hall-petch Relation
$$\sigma_y=\sigma_0+kD^{-0.5}$$
### Solid solution strengthening

> [!important] Impurities distort lattice & generate lattice strain that act as barriers to dislocation motion
- Partially cancels out compressive/tensile strength of dislocations
### Strain hardening (cold work)
![[References/Images/image 22 3.png|image 22 3.png]]
Yield strength increase
Tensile strength increase
Ductility decrease
$$\% {CW} = \frac{A_0-A_d}{A_0}*100$$
$A_0=\text{initial cross section}$
$A_d=\text{resultant cross section}$
- Dislocations multiply significantly and entangle during cold work → reduced slipping
- Large deformation in cold working can make material too brittle
    - **Intermittent annealing** (heating and holding at high temperatures) can restore ductility for further deformations
![[References/Images/image 23 3.png|image 23 3.png]]
Recovery — reduction of dislocation density by annihilation
Results of higher temperatures:
- easier atomic diffusion → atom can move to fill area of tension
- larger vacancy concentration → allow dislocations to climb into slip plane and annihilate
Recrystallisation
New grains formed with:
- low dislocation density — small in size — consume and replace parent cold worked grains
- Recrystallization temperature — Temp which it completes in 1h exactly
    - $0.3T_m<T_R<0.6T_m$
    - Decreases with increasing %CW & increasing purity
    - **Hot working** happens above this temp
Grain growth — small grain shrink and assimilate with larger grains
